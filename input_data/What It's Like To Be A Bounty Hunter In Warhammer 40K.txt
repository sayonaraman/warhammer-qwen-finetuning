The galaxy would probably be a more efficient place if we all shared a unified purpose and perspective. But efficiency is not the same as humanity. Unity without choice is not transcendence but slavery. and sometimes the chaos of individual free will is preferable to the perfect order of collective consciousness. These are the kinds of thoughts that keep bounty hunters awake at night. The understanding that the monsters we hunt are often trying to solve real problems, just using methods that would destroy everything we value about being human. Six months later, as I sit in my cramped quarters aboard this merchant hauler, I find myself wondering what other xenotech artifacts are waiting to be discovered. How many research teams are studying alien consciousness enhancement technologies right now? How many colonies might be experimenting with cognitive synthesis and collective unity? The Jericho Reach Crusade recovered 40 different Zenotech artifacts. I helped destroy one. That leaves 39 still in Imperial hands, being studied by people who believe they can safely harness alien technologies for human benefit. History suggests they are wrong. But that is a concern for another day, another hunt. Right now, I have a new contract waiting on Sintilla, a deserter colonel who sold his regiment's battle plans to Chaos Cultists. Simple retrieval job, minimal risk, clean extraction expected within ten solar days. I should know better than to believe it will be that simple. In this business, the moment someone tells you a job is simple, you can be certain it will try to kill you in ways you never imagined. But that is the nature of the hunt. the price we pay for walking the dark paths between stars. My name is Varen Locke. I am a sanctioned bounty hunter, and tomorrow I will begin tracking another monster through the shadows of the Imperium. Because that is what I do. That is who I am. And the galaxy has no shortage of monsters that need killing. You learn to smell fear before you see it. But sometimes, in the quiet moments between hunts, you realize that the fear you smell is your own. Fear that the next monster might be too clever, too dangerous, too seductive in its promises of transcendence. Fear that someday you might find yourself agreeing with the creatures you hunt. That is the price of walking the hunting paths, not death, which would be simple, but the constant risk of becoming the very thing you have spent your life trying to destroy. In the end, we are all just one choice away from becoming monsters ourselves. The only difference is whether we make that choice willingly, or have it made for us by forces beyond our understanding.
