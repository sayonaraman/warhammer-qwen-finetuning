They say one never notices the moment of transformation. The line between what you were and what you have become blurs like blood in water. I remember when my hands were still human. I was once Kailvor, planetary governor of Marinus IV, a world of reasonable prosperity and unremarkable loyalty. The Administratum reports had always marked us as compliant. Our tithes were paid with minimal delay. Our recruitment quotas were met without complaint. Generations of my family had governed with quiet competence. None among us had ever sought glory. We simply existed in service to the Golden Throne, as countless billions do across the stars. None would have predicted my fall. I certainly did not. The first corruption is so subtle that you welcome it as clarity. It began during the Festival of Compliance, a drab ceremonial week where we celebrated five centuries of unbroken imperial rule. The skies were overcast that season, endless clouds of gray that seemed to press down upon the spires of our modest hive city. I stood upon the balcony of the gubernatorial palace, looking out at the masses gathered below, their faces turned upward in mindless devotion. The sight should have filled me with pride. Instead, I felt nothing but contempt. How many generations had we spent in servitude to a corpse god who offered nothing in return? How many of our sons and daughters had been fed into the meat-grinder of imperial wars, fought on distant worlds for reasons none could articulate? The questions came unbidden, thoughts I had never before allowed myself to entertain, yet there they were, sudden and insistent, like a wound that had always existed beneath my skin but only now began to bleed. That night I could not sleep, my mind raced with possibilities I had never considered, visions of a different world, a different future, plagued me. In the darkness of my chambers, I heard a voice, Soft at first, barely distinguishable from my own thoughts. It spoke of potential, of power waiting to be claimed, of freedom from the crushing weight of imperial rule. I told no one of these thoughts. In the following days, I performed my duties as expected. I reviewed production reports, authorized resource allocations, and presided over minor legal disputes. But beneath the surface, something had changed. I began to see the flaws in the system I had served for so long. The inefficiency, the waste, the cruelty masquerading as necessity. The voice grew stronger. It spoke of ancient powers that existed before the Imperium, before the Emperor, gods who rewarded ambition rather than blind obedience, gods who saw potential in men like me. I did not immediately succumb. For months I resisted, clinging to the beliefs that had defined my life. I increased my attendance at Imperial services, I commissioned a new statue of the emperor for the central plaza. I ordered public executions of suspected heretics, watching their bodies burn with an intensity that disturbed even my most zealous advisers, but resistance only made the voice stronger. It began to show me things, visions that came when I closed my eyes. I saw myself leading armies across the stars. I saw my name spoken with reverence and fear. I saw power beyond anything the Imperium could offer. The change was gradual enough that I could pretend it was not happening. Small compromises, minor indulgences. A blind eye turned to certain activities within my domain. Private investigations into ancient texts that should have been destroyed. Secret communications with individuals whose names never appeared in official records. The first physical manifestation of my corruption appeared on the night of the winter solstice.
