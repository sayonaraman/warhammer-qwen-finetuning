In the deepest vaults of terror, beneath layers of ferrocrete and forgotten prayers, men and women sit in chambers so quiet that the sound of their own heartbeat becomes a thunderclap. They are called Echo Men, though few know this name, and fewer still understand what it means. For forty thousand years they have performed a duty so terrible that even the Imperium, in all its casual cruelty, considers it a punishment worse than execution. The administratum keeps no official record of their service, the ecclesiarchy offers no prayers for their souls. Even the commissariat, those arbiters of imperial discipline, speak of echo-men only in whispers, as if acknowledging their existence might somehow invoke their fate. Yet without these broken souls, the emperor's final judgment could never reach the worlds that need it most. To understand the Echo Men, one must first understand the nature of Exterminatus itself. It is not merely a weapon, though weapons are involved. It is not merely a judgment, though judgment is passed. Exterminatus is a conversation between the living and the dying, a final exchange that spans the void between worlds and carries with it the weight of absolute annihilation. And like all conversations, it must be heard. When the Inquisition declares a world beyond salvation, when virus bombs fall like rain from heaven, when cyclonic torpedoes crack continents like eggs, the dying planet screams. Not metaphorically, not symbolically. The planet screams, and its voice carries across the warp on frequencies that can drive a man mad in minutes. These screams contain more than mere agony. They contain last words, final confessions, desperate prayers to gods who will not answer. They contain the collective death-rattle of billions compressed into a signal that must be received, decoded, and transcribed for the imperial record. This is the work of the Echo Men.
