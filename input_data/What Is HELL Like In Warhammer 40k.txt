These new arrivals were different from the voluntary suicides we had grown accustomed to processing. Some had been partially processed through the grinding mechanisms of the forges before being thrown down to us. Others showed evidence of chemical treatment that made their flesh toxic to consume. More disturbing still were the bodies that had been modified with crude augmentics, devices that served no functional purpose but were designed to explode when disturbed. The first of these killed three of our scavengers before we learned to recognize the signs. The overseers were not simply trying to deny us resources, but were actively weaponizing the dead against us. They know, she gasped, her voice barely audible above the constant dripping of moisture from the cavern walls. The overseers know about the community down here. They had survived centuries in the Gulf by adapting to every challenge it presented, but this was different. This was not a problem that could be solved through scavenging or ritual or the economics of desperation. This was annihilation, pure and simple. Using techniques developed through their rituals of processing, they could create new forms of life adapted specifically to the conditions of the hell pits. They retained enough human intelligence to operate the machinery of the hell pits, but were driven by imperatives that served the interests of the Gulf community rather than imperial authority. I watched this transformation with a mixture of fascination and revulsion, understanding that I was witnessing the birth of something unprecedented in imperial history. The collapse of the Gulf, when it finally came, was anticlimactic.
